**Short answer: consistency is the most important factor in building muscle — more important than having the perfect program.** Muscle is built from training volume accumulated over months, not from any single flawless session. A good-enough plan you follow week after week will out-build an optimal plan you abandon. And the research on habits is reassuring: missing the occasional session doesn't undo your progress. The lifters who get results aren't the ones with the smartest routine — they're the ones still training when everyone else has quit.

## Does consistency really beat the perfect program?

It does, and it isn't close. Online debates about the optimal split, rep range, or exercise selection imply these choices make or break your results. For the vast majority of lifters, they don't. The differences between sensible programs are small. The difference between training consistently and training sporadically is enormous.

This is the same logic as nutrition: the best diet is the one you can stick to. The best training program is the one you'll actually run for months without burning out, getting bored, or finding it impossible to fit around your life. Optimisation only matters once consistency is handled — and most people never get that far because they keep chasing the perfect plan instead of repeating a good one.

## Progress is accumulated, not earned in a session

Muscle growth is driven by total training volume built up over time, supported by recovery (see [how recovery drives strength](/guides/how-recovery-drives-strength)). No single workout moves the needle much — it's the *sum* of hundreds of sessions that does. That reframes what a workout is: not a make-or-break event, but one deposit in a long-running account.

It also explains why the occasional mediocre or missed session barely registers. One average workout among a hundred good ones is statistically irrelevant. What matters is that the deposits keep landing, week after week, month after month.

## One missed day won't break you

The streak in the logbook above tells the real story — a long run of marked-off training days, built one session at a time. But the most useful finding on habits is the one that takes the pressure off: when researchers tracked people forming new daily behaviours, **missing a single opportunity didn't meaningfully derail the process** (Lally et al., 2010, *European Journal of Social Psychology*). The same study found it took a **median of 66 days** for a behaviour to become automatic — with some people taking far longer.

So two things are true at once: building the habit takes a while, and one slip won't undo it. The failure mode isn't the missed Tuesday — it's letting the missed Tuesday become a missed week, then a missed month. Don't break the chain twice in a row, and you keep the streak alive in every way that matters.

## How to actually stay consistent

Consistency comes from systems, not willpower. Motivation is unreliable; structure isn't. The things that work:

- **Train at the same times** so sessions become automatic rather than decisions you re-make each day.
- **Lower the friction** — bag packed, plan decided, gym chosen for convenience over perfection.
- **Make it visible** — a streak, a log, a calendar you tick. Seeing the chain build is its own motivation, and it's harder to break something you can see.
- **Scale to what you can sustain.** Three days a week you never miss beats five you keep abandoning.
- **Judge yourself by the month, not the day.** A bad week inside a good month is a good month.

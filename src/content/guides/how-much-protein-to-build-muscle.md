To build muscle, most lifters should eat **around 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of bodyweight per day** — roughly 0.7 g per pound. For a 70 kg (154 lb) lifter that's about 110 g a day; for 90 kg (198 lb), about 145 g. There's a small additional benefit up to roughly **2.2 g/kg**, and almost none beyond that. Hit that range consistently, spread across your meals, and you've handled the part of nutrition that actually drives muscle growth.

Everything else — timing, shakes, the "anabolic window" — is a rounding error next to simply hitting your daily total. Here's how to do that without overthinking it.

## How much protein per day to build muscle?

The clearest answer comes from a 2018 meta-analysis by Morton and colleagues, which pooled 49 studies and over 1,800 people. Muscle gains increased with protein intake up to about **1.6 g/kg/day**, then flattened out — the upper edge of the benefit sat near **2.2 g/kg**. Above that, more protein didn't build more muscle.

In real numbers, aiming for ~1.6 g/kg works out to roughly:

- **60 kg (132 lb):** ~95 g/day
- **70 kg (154 lb):** ~110 g/day
- **80 kg (176 lb):** ~130 g/day
- **90 kg (198 lb):** ~145 g/day

One useful detail: trained lifters tend to get *more* out of hitting their protein than beginners do, so the longer you've been lifting, the more this matters. Treat the number as a floor to reach most days — not a target to chase to the last gram.

## How much protein per meal — is there a cap?

You've probably heard the body can only "use" 20–30 g of protein per meal and the rest is wasted. That's a myth, or at least badly oversimplified. The 20–25 g figure came from studies feeding *fast-digesting* protein (like whey isolate) on its own. Real meals — protein alongside fats, carbs and fibre — digest more slowly, so the amino acids arrive over a longer window.

The practical guideline from Schoenfeld and Aragon (2018) is to aim for about **0.4 g/kg per meal across at least four meals**, which lands you at 1.6 g/kg for the day (or up to ~0.55 g/kg per meal to reach 2.2). For most people that's simply **30–40 g of protein per meal, three or four times a day** — no precision required, and no protein "wasted."

## Does protein timing matter?

Far less than the supplement industry implies. The old idea of a narrow post-workout "anabolic window" — eat within 30 minutes or lose your gains — hasn't held up. The window is measured in **hours**, not minutes.

What matters is your **total intake across the day**. If you've eaten a normal protein-containing meal in the few hours before or after training, you're covered. A shake immediately after lifting is convenient, not magic. Spread your protein sensibly across the day and the timing takes care of itself.

## How much protein when cutting?

When you're in a calorie deficit to lose fat, protein becomes *more* important, not less. Eating enough protein while dieting helps preserve the muscle you've built rather than burning it for fuel. This is the time to sit at the **higher end of the range — around 2.2 g/kg or a touch above** — because adequate protein is what protects lean mass when overall energy is low.

## Which protein sources are best?

Quality comes down to whether a source delivers a complete set of amino acids — particularly **leucine**, the one that flips the muscle-building switch. Animal sources (whey, eggs, dairy, meat, fish) are complete and leucine-rich, which is why they're efficient. Plant sources work too, but individually they're often lower in one amino acid or another, so plant-based lifters should **combine sources** (e.g. rice and beans, or soy and grains) and lean a little higher on total intake.

Whole foods first; a shake is just a convenient way to top up the total when whole food isn't practical.

## The number isn't the hard part

Hitting 1.6 g/kg isn't complicated. Hitting it *every day* is — and that's the bit that actually decides whether you build muscle. Protein is one of the recovery inputs that turn your training into strength (see [how recovery drives strength](/guides/how-recovery-drives-strength) for the full picture).

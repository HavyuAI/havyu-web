**Short answer: aim for roughly 3.7 litres of total water a day if you're a man and 2.7 litres if you're a woman, then add fluid around training.** Those totals, from the US National Academies, include the water in your food. The reason it matters for lifters specifically is that hydration directly affects performance: losing even 2% of your body weight in fluid is enough to measurably reduce strength and power. Hydration isn't a wellness afterthought — it's a training variable.

## What dehydration actually does to your lifting

Muscle is about 75% water, and fluid balance affects everything from blood volume to nerve signalling to temperature control. When you're under-hydrated, all of that degrades.

In controlled research, dehydration of just **2-3% of body mass** significantly reduced maximal voluntary contraction force, power output, and resistance-exercise performance (Judelson et al., 2007, *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*). For an 80 kg lifter, 2% is only about 1.6 kg of fluid — easily lost across a hot session or a day of under-drinking. The drop comes from both reduced muscle contractile capacity and reduced central drive — your nervous system simply can't recruit the muscle as hard.

There's a recovery cost too. A companion study found that training in a dehydrated state **raised the cortisol response** to resistance exercise (Judelson et al., 2008, *Journal of Applied Physiology*). More cortisol around training is the opposite of what you want when the goal is to build, which is part of why hydration sits inside the broader recovery picture covered in [how recovery drives strength](/guides/how-recovery-drives-strength).

## How much water per day — and around training

Use the daily totals as your baseline, then layer training fluid on top:

- **Daily baseline:** ~3.7 L total water (men) / ~2.7 L (women), food included. In practice that's roughly 2.5-3 litres of actual drinking for most active people.
- **Before training:** ~400-600 ml in the 2-3 hours beforehand, so you start topped up rather than playing catch-up.
- **During training:** sip 150-300 ml every 15-20 minutes. Don't wait until you're thirsty — thirst lags behind actual fluid loss.
- **After training:** replace what you lost. If you weigh noticeably less after a hard, sweaty session, that's fluid, not fat — drink to bring it back.

Hot weather, heavy sweating, caffeine, and high altitude all increase your needs. Bigger lifters need more than the averages above.

## Signs you're not drinking enough

You don't need to track millilitres obsessively. A few simple signals tell you most of what you need:

- **Urine colour** — pale straw is the target; dark yellow means you're behind.
- **Thirst** — a sign you're already mildly dehydrated, not a starting gun.
- **Performance** — feeling flat, weak, or unable to hit your usual reps can be a hydration problem before it's a training one.
- **Headaches or cramping** — often fluid and electrolyte related, especially late in a session.

## Do you need electrolytes?

For a typical gym session under 60-90 minutes, water is enough. Electrolytes — sodium in particular — earn their place when you sweat heavily, train in heat, or go long, because sodium helps you hold onto the fluid you drink rather than passing it straight through. A pinch of salt in your bottle or an electrolyte tab does the job; you rarely need a sugary sports drink for normal lifting.
